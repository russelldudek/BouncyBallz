What's Happening? The Physics Behind the Bounce

When you drop a ball, gravity pulls it down, converting Potential Energy (PE) (energy due to height) into Kinetic Energy (KE) (energy due to motion).

When the ball hits the ground, it undergoes a collision. In a perfectly Elastic Collision, all the KE is momentarily stored as elastic potential energy in the deformed ball and then released, converting back into KE, causing the ball to bounce back to its original height (Coefficient of Restitution, COR = 1). In reality, collisions are often Inelastic â€“ some KE is lost as heat, sound, or permanent deformation. This lost energy is shown as the "Energy Lost" bar.

The Coefficient of Restitution (COR) is a number between 0 and 1 that tells us how 'bouncy' a collision is. A COR near 1 means highly elastic (bouncy), and a COR near 0 means highly inelastic (like dropping a ball of clay). The height of a bounce (if dropped from rest) is proportional to the square of the COR: `Bounce Height = CORÂ² * Drop Height`.

Adding an initial downward push gives the ball more initial KE. To bounce back to the original height, the ball needs enough KE *after* the bounce to convert back to the original PE. Because energy is lost in an inelastic collision (COR < 1), you need to give the ball *more* initial KE before the bounce than the PE you want it to reach *after* the bounce.

And the explosion? If the initial downward velocity (and thus KE) is too high, the force of impact during the collision can exceed the physical limits of the ball's material, causing it to... well, explode! (In our simulation, at least!)
